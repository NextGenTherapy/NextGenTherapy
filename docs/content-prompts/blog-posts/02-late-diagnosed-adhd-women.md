## Angle

This post acknowledges the complex emotional experience of receiving an ADHD diagnosis in adulthood—particularly for women who have spent years masking, compensating, and blaming themselves. Andreea writes as an affirmative therapist who doesn't try to "fix" neurodivergence but helps clients process the grief of lost years alongside the relief of finally understanding themselves. The post validates the reader's experience while positioning psychodynamic therapy as a space to explore identity, not just symptoms.

## Structure

1. **Opening hook** — The moment of diagnosis: relief, grief, anger, confusion—often all at once
2. **Why women get missed** — Internalised presentation, masking, being labelled "emotional" or "disorganised" instead of neurodivergent
3. **The grief that comes with late diagnosis** — Mourning the years of struggle, the relationships affected, the self-blame
4. **The relief underneath** — Finally having language for your experience. Not broken, just different.
5. **Why therapy after diagnosis matters** — Diagnosis is information, not healing. Processing the emotional impact takes time.
6. **Affirmative therapy: what it actually means** — Not trying to make you "more neurotypical." Working with your brain, not against it.
7. **The room** — Mention sensory-aware environment: soft lighting, stimming welcomed, no pressure to mask
8. **Closing** — Invitation to explore neurodiversity page or book a call
